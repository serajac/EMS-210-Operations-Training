Course Overview: Hazardous Materials Awareness

Learning Objective: By the end of this course, you will be able to recognize hazardous materials incidents, identify substances using various methods, understand health risks, and implement appropriate response procedures at the awareness level.

What are Hazardous Materials?

Hazardous materials (hazmat) are any items or agents that have the potential to cause harm to humans, animals, or the environment, either through direct contact or through interaction with other factors.

Vehicle Crashes

Transportation accidents involving trucks, rail cars, or other vehicles carrying hazardous materials.

Storage Mishaps

Accidents at manufacturing facilities, warehouses, or storage areas containing chemicals.

Illicit Operations

Clandestine drug manufacturing labs that create toxic chemical hazards.

Terrorism Acts

Intentional release of hazardous substances as weapons of mass destruction.

Chemical Mixing

Accidental combination of household chemicals, pesticides, or industrial products.

Natural Disasters

Secondary hazmat releases caused by earthquakes, floods, or severe weather.

Key Regulations

Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act (1986)

Established requirements for emergency planning and reporting of hazmat-related incidents. Facility operators must notify:

  • Local fire department
  • Local emergency managers
  • State emergency response commission

HAZWOPER Standards (OSHA)

At the awareness level, responders must be able to:

  • Evaluate and identify hazmat/WMD incidents
  • Call for appropriate resources
  • Identify substances using placards and reference materials
  • Gather information from the Emergency Response Guidebook
  • Maintain safe perimeters around incidents
Important: NFPA 470 addresses competencies for EMS personnel at hazmat scenes, including scene analysis, ALS delivery, treatment planning, and post-decontamination care.

Hazmat Identification Methods

Informal Product Identification

Initial assessment methods that can be performed from a safe distance:

Visual Inspection

Use binoculars to observe from safe distance. Look for vapor clouds, unusual containers, leakage, and environmental clues.

Verbal Reports

Information from witnesses, facility personnel, or other first responders about the incident.

Occupancy Type

Consider facility type: chemical plant, gas station, laboratory, manufacturing, or agricultural facility.

Incident Location

Geographic location and specific area within a facility can indicate likely hazards present.

Vehicle Types

Tank trucks, rail cars, cargo containers, and specialized transport vehicles carrying hazmat.

Container Characteristics

Size, shape, material, and markings on containers provide clues about contents and hazards.

Environmental Clues

Dead vegetation, unusual odors, vapor clouds, discolored surfaces, or dead animals.

Human Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of exposed individuals can indicate types of chemical hazards present.

Formal Product Identification Systems

Six major labeling systems used historically for hazmat identification:

DOT System

Department of Transportation - Primary system for hazmat transportation with placards and UN/NA numbers.

NFPA System

National Fire Protection Association - Diamond-shaped labels showing fire, health, reactivity, and special hazards.

OSHA HazCom

Occupational Safety and Health Administration Hazard Communication Standard for workplace safety.

GHS System

Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals - International standard.

Key Information Sources

Placards and Shipping Papers

  • Display United Nations/North American (UN/NA) identification numbers
  • Located near vehicle operator (truck cab, ship bridge, aircraft cockpit)
  • Multiple chemical agents may share the same UN/NA number
  • Always refer to specific chemical name when possible, not just number

Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

  • Required by OSHA for each chemical produced, stored, or used in United States
  • Supplied by manufacturer with detailed safety information
  • Contains handling, storage, and emergency response information
  • Should not be used as sole source of chemical information
  • Must consult medical direction, poison control, or appropriate authorities
